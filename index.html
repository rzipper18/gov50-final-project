The Relationship Between Poverty Rate and Economic Ideology in Congress

Robert Zipper

Project Thoughts

In American politics, it is common knowledge that the two main political parties have divergent economic ideologies, specifically regarding redistribution of income by the federal government. The Democratic Party has generally been more receptive than the Republican Party to federal income-redistribution programs, including poverty relief programs.

However, voters do not only vote according to their economic interests – increasingly, they have also been voting according to their cultural and other related ideological interests. Therefore, many voters in high-poverty regions of the U.S. do not vote for Democratic candidates who are more inclined to support economic policies (e.g., poverty relief programs) that would benefit these voters. As a result, their representatives may not be supporting the enactment of policies that would reduce poverty in these communities.

For this project, I would like to examine the extent to which a U.S. congressional district’s poverty rate is correlated with the economic voting ideology of that district’s representative in Congress. I plan to use the DW-NOMINATE (DW-N) scores of members of Congress as a way to measure their economic ideology on the left-right spectrum, based on their voting records. A lower DW-N score suggests that a representative is more economically liberal and thus more likely to support federal anti-poverty programs. I expect to see a positive correlation, but I do not think it will be a strong one, given the geographical and partisan heterogeneity of high-poverty regions across the United States.

Project Proposal

Do members of Congress serving districts with high rates of poverty support more progressive economic policies? In this study, I plan to examine the extent to which a U.S. congressional district’s poverty rate correlates with its U.S. Representative’s economic ideology, as measured by the representative’s voting record in Congress. It is reasonable to assume that members of Congress support policies that are in the best economic interests of their constituents. Therefore, I hypothesize that members of Congress representing districts with high rates of poverty are more likely to support progressive economic policies because such policies tend to benefit poorer Americans. For example, progressive economic ideology is associated with greater spending on anti-poverty programs, which, of course, disproportionately benefit those living in high-poverty regions. My sample is comprised of the poverty rates for each U.S. congressional district compiled by the Food Research and Action Center, based on the 2017 American Community Survey. My unit of analysis is a congressional district, and my explanatory variable of interest is the poverty rate, which is measured as the proportion of households in a district with an annual income below the federal poverty line. My outcome variable of interest is a U.S. Representative’s DW-NOMINATE score (dimension 1), which measures a representative’s economic ideology on a scale from -1 to 1, with -1 being the progressive end and 1 being the conservative end of the spectrum. These scores are calculated based on the representative’s voting record in Congress. If I observe a negative correlation between a congressional district’s poverty rate and its representative’s DW-NOMINATE score, this would provide support for my hypothesis, since it would demonstrate that members of Congress representing districts with high poverty rates tend to be more economically progressive (i.e., have lower DW-NOMINATE scores). If, on the other hand, I observe a positive correlation between a district’s poverty rate and its representative’s DW-NOMINATE score, this would provide evidence against my hypothesis. When I run my regression of U.S. Representative DW-NOMINATE score on the congressional district poverty rate variable, a negative, significant coefficient would indicate support for my hypothesis. I will refer to an increase in the DW-NOMINATE score as an increase in U.S. Representative “economic conservatism.”

Final Report

Introduction

To what extent is a U.S. congressional district’s poverty rate correlated with its Representative’s economic ideology? Representatives with progressive economic ideologies are more likely to support policies which disproportionately benefit Americans below the poverty line, such as anti-poverty programs or the distribution of income to the poor. However, a Representative’s economic ideology is also strongly influenced by political party affiliation. Representatives from the Democratic Party are generally more supportive of progressive economic policies than their Republican counterparts. I hypothesize is that there is a statistically significant correlation between a congressional district’s poverty rate and its U.S. Representative’s economic ideology – that is, districts with higher rates of poverty are significantly more likely to be represented by economic progressives. Furthermore, I hypothesize that this correlation holds true at the party level. In other words, I expect to see a negative correlation between district poverty rate and economic conservatism among Republicans and among Democrats. What makes this hypothesis interesting is that many high-poverty districts in the United States, especially those located in rural areas, are represented by Republicans who are generally reluctant to support progressive economic policies that would disproportionately benefit their poor constituents. This report examines how strong the relationship between the poverty rate and representative economic ideology is in the U.S. Congress.

Data

The data referenced in this report was extracted from two sources: 1) the Food Research and Action Center’s U.S. congressional district-level poverty data (based on the 2017 American Community Survey) and 2) the DW-NOMINATE (DW-N) scores for members of the 114th Congress (2015-17).

In this report, the independent variable is a U.S. congressional district’s 2017 poverty rate, or the percentage of households in a district below the federal poverty line as of 2017. According to the U.S. Department of Health and Human Services, a household of one was below the federal poverty line if that individual’s income was less than $12,060 in 2017. A family of four was below the poverty line in 2017 if their household income was less than $24,600 that year.

The dependent variable in this report is a DW-N score for a member of the 114th Congress. A DW-N score is calculated based on a U.S. Representative’s voting record on economic issues while in Congress. A DW-N score closer to -1 is associated with economic progressivism, whereas a DW-N score closer to 1 is associated with economic conservatism.

Notwithstanding, it is still unreasonable to conclude that there is a causal effect between congressional district poverty rate and U.S. Representative DW-N score. To determine whether there is a causal effect, one must use multiple regression to control for one crucial potential confounding variable: party affiliation.

The estimated coefficient for the born variable is highly statistically significant as well. Holding congressional district poverty rate constant, a 1-year increase in the born year, meaning a 1-year decrease in U.S. Representative age, predicts an increase in the DW-N score by 0.01. In other words, younger U.S. Representatives are more likely to be economically conservative. This finding is inconsistent with the hypothesis that older U.S. Representatives are more economically conservative.

Below is the result of running the regression controlling for party affiliation as well as representative birth year. With a p-value of 0.15, the poverty rate coefficient is no longer statistically significant. This finding demonstrates that a congressional district’s poverty rate likely does not have a causal effect the U.S. Representative’s economic ideology. Unsurprisingly, there is a highly statistically significant relationship between party affiliation and economic ideology. These results suggest that representatives’ party affiliation strongly influences their economic ideology as measured by the DW-N score. While this demonstrates that there is likely not a causal relationship between congressional district poverty rate and representative economic ideology, it does not diminish the fact that, as hypothesized, there is a noticeable negative correlation between congressional district poverty rate and representative economic conservatism.

Among Republicans, however, the relationship between district poverty rate and DW-N score is quite different. The correlation is actually positive at 0.05, meaning that Republicans representing high-poverty districts are slightly less likely to support progressive economic policies. However, with a poverty rate coefficient of 0.454, this relationship is not statistically significant. Nonetheless, this finding is inconsistent with my hypothesis, which predicts a significant negative correlation between congressional district poverty rate and representative DW-N score.

Conclusion

I hypothesized that there is a significant negative correlation between congressional district poverty rate and U.S. Representative DW-N score. Consistent with my hypothesis, the correlation was found to be -0.21 and the regression between these two variables is statistically significant. The correlation remained statistically significant when controlling for U.S. Representative year of birth. Contrary to my hypothesis, there is a statistically significant negative correlation between representative age and DW-N score. As expected, when political party affiliation was added to the regression, the relationship between district poverty rate and DW-N score was no longer statistically significant. The likely reason is that representatives’ economic ideology is usually strongly influenced by their party affiliation. Therefore, one cannot infer a causal relationship between congressional district poverty rate and U.S. Representative economic ideology. Consistent with my hypothesis, there is a statistically significant negative correlation between poverty rate and DW-N score among Democrats. However, contrary to my hypothesis, there is no such association among Republicans. One limitation of my analysis is that it does not show that congressional district poverty level has a causal effect on U.S. Representative DW-N score. The negative correlations observed between these two variables are likely due to the fact that there are more high-poverty Democratic districts than Republican districts. However, it is worth emphasizing that this study did not hypothesize a causal relationship between these two variables. My analysis could be improved by adding more variables to the regression to control for other potential confounders. I believe it would strengthen my analysis to control for variables such as whether a U.S. Representative grew up in poverty and how long the representative has served in Congress.
